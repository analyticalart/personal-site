## Chapter 1.1 — Platform Engineering is a thing?

DevOps has a lot of sub-types, mainly SRE which was under the guise of DevOps has been recognised as a separate role, but there are more which are yet to be completely dug out. Not that they don't exist as separate roles, but they seem to exist as DevOps based on how serious the company is to match the title and the job function.

These roles still exist in the DevOps junk drawer in a lot of companies-
- Cloud Engineering (vendor lock-in but make it career based)
- Platform Engineering (I am part of this)
- Product specific DevOps (the thing where disaster recovery tutorials are put to use)

---

I remember when I was hired into my first job as a DevOps engineer, I was expecting somewhat of a brutal war zone-ish on-call rotations, monitoring dashboards with 3 monitors **minimum**, resolving urgent tickets and silencing sev 1 alerts before they get escalated to the heavens. Thank god for SREs and in my case the production on-call engineers on that day's roster.

Rather, it was quite peaceful actually, our work was more focused on making the DevEx more efficient and adding a layer of abstraction on the infrastructure so the devs won't have to get a CKAD or AWS/Azure/GCP/Alibaba(???) certifications. We developed internal tools, tools that super powered our devs so that they could focus on studying DS&A for interviews in their after work hours. 
One of the most coolest implementations done by my team was a Kubernetes custom controller? based on the Kopf operator. Though my brain was too smooth to let the information stick when I discovered it, I realized this is based on something called GitOps. 

The infrastructure of our internal tooling for K8s was interesting, we had CRDs deploying helm charts acting as plugins on spoke clusters which was deployed through our main core/hub cluster which was running a deployment of the custom controller. Jargony? Imagine a spider, the body being the central function unit having all the default plugins and the deployment pod of the controller, with every leg end was attached a cluster. The source of truth only mattered if it was on the web, if the weave was missing, the reconciliation loop nuked any temporary changes.

This technical difficulty of a multi-cluster architecture (called spidey henceforth) was not something that clicked. During my internship I had worked as a backend Java dev on a website that was actually a monolith application, no docker, no container, no touching cloud either (only the tech lead was allowed to nuke things I suppose?) with only the knowledge that I was allowed to deploy every change by hitting build on Jenkins and then SSH into a machine to view logs because Jboss's local run would hog my laptop's RAM and make it into a lap heater.

One of the ways I started to slowly grasp the spidey was through gaslighting myself into thinking I knew what a component did. See spidey was built on GitOps, that meant if we were to cut spidey up, we would at least see 100 bitbucket repos. Connecting the dots just was not it, the controller in spidey's body was complicated, so everytime I had to work on a plugin fixture (CRD being the plugin) because it did not work, I only did the steps I was told. Eventually as the plugin itself started to make sense, I slowly started to move to what was in the repo that was powering the plugin. 

My brain refuses to raw dog technical documentation, I am not sure how to put it but seeing thick (metaphorically) sidebar with 1000s of sections with every page having a read more filled with jargon that does not even slide off but shoots back at 180 from my brain is something I struggle with, **deeply**. Having analogies to architectures would have been fun, but not everyone learns that way or makes connections that way, this is the reason why I started to feel resentment in understanding complex code pretty soon. 

So what did I do? Nothing actually, sometimes life gives you lemons and you have to squeeze them into your own eyes. I started to realize that understanding complex code did not mean rote learning syntax of a language or knowing how to delete an image in a container running on a pod through crictl (yes, this made me feel superior for exactly 2 hours, then I realized this was not supposed to be entertained as an on-call support ticket). It comes with having a high level understanding of how the system connects. The spidey being a spidey was not a fact I was aware of nor was it presented to me that way, I was getting brain zaps on the intution but I figured this out only a few months back, being able to finally coin it, that it is a hub and spoke MCA. See, sometimes these kind of revealations feel quite like a miracle, especially when you are in the start of the [dunning kruger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dunning%E2%80%93Kruger_effect) curve where even knowing one command makes you feel like a god, if not addressed early eventually leads to the [confirmation bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_memory#Mandela_effect) (gotcha!) about feeling like you just know everything and don't tend to fact check it. 


Once you get a high level overview on what your code means, understanding the foundations actually starts to feel interesting. As someone who would watch a video or two on helm or k8s and somehow manage to not retain a single word until it mattered to my brain, I struggled a lot in understanding how the components of our systems connected. Despite having a wild internship arc, one thing that made me want to show up to the boss fight with Jboss was because I could see the changes immediately, the site would be live with the code being visible. However this was not the case with my role. That is probably when I realized, by understanding a bigger picture of what the code was meant to do it started to also make sense as to why it mattered to the end users. Yeah, this thing is called user empathy, surprising that it could even come up in a cold and soulless job like DevOps.
